‚ĚĮ What is "streaming data" or "streaming-first"? Isn't abrupt drift technically 
   called "changepoint" ?                                                       

‚Źļ Good questions, both get at terminology that's used loosely in this field.

  "Streaming data" / "streaming-first"

  Streaming = data arrives one observation at a time, in order, and you process
  each point as it comes ‚ÄĒ you don't have the whole series in memory. Think of a
   live sensor feed or stock ticks: you see x_t, react, then see x_{t+1}. The
  opposite is batch/offline, where you have the entire array up front and can
  look forwards and backwards freely.

  So a streaming-first library (like river) is built around the assumption that
  you feed it points one at a time and it updates its internal state
  incrementally ‚ÄĒ detector.update(x) returns "drift / no drift" right now, using
   only what it's seen so far. It can't peek at the future.

  An offline library (like ruptures) takes the whole series at once and finds
  the best split points by considering all the data globally.

  This matters for your project: if your diagnostic tool gets a finished CSV,
  you're in the offline world (ruptures, Frouros' batch detectors). If it's
  meant to mimic monitoring a live pipeline, you're streaming (river). Most
  "junior analyst diagnoses a dataset handed to them" framings are offline.

  "Isn't abrupt drift just a changepoint?"

  You're basically right, and the sloppiness is real. The distinction is about
  what changed, and the two communities use different words:

  - Changepoint detection (statistics/signal processing) cares about a change in
   the distribution of the observed series itself ‚ÄĒ usually a shift in mean or
  variance of x_t. Abrupt, instantaneous. "The sensor reading jumped at t=500."
  - Concept drift (streaming ML) cares about a change in the relationship P(y | 
  X) ‚ÄĒ the mapping from features to target. The input may look identical, but
  the correct label changed. "Same weather features, but now they predict rain
  instead of sun."

  So:
  - An abrupt changepoint in a univariate series ‚Čą abrupt drift. Same thing,
  different vocabulary.
  - But concept drift is broader ‚ÄĒ it can be gradual (slow rotation of a
  decision boundary over thousands of points), or it can happen with no visible 
  change in the raw series at all, which a changepoint detector would completely
   miss.

  1. Drift vs changepoint ‚ÄĒ how to tell abrupt from gradual

  You're right that forecast-error degradation is a shared symptom ‚ÄĒ it can't be
   the discriminator. The distinction has to come from the temporal profile of 
  the change itself, not from the fact that error went up. A few complementary
  ways to separate them:

  (a) The shape of the transition. This is the core signal.
  - A changepoint is localized: the level/slope jumps within a narrow window,
  and before/after it the series is stable. If you compute a rolling mean, you
  see a step.
  - Drift is distributed: the level/slope moves continuously over many points
  with no single break. The rolling mean ramps.

  So the question becomes "is the change concentrated at one index, or spread
  across an interval?" ‚ÄĒ and that's measurable.

  (b) How error degrades over time, not just that it does.
  - After a changepoint, forecast error jumps then plateaus (the model is wrong
  by a fixed offset until refit). A step in the residual mean.
  - Under drift, error grows progressively ‚ÄĒ the gap between the stale model and
   the moving series widens monotonically. A ramp/trend in the residuals.

  So the temporal profile of the residual error is itself the discriminator,
  even though both "degrade."

  (c) Detector behavior / cross-checking. This is the pragmatic version, and it
  fits your two-team split nicely:
  - A changepoint detector (ruptures/PELT, BOCD) will return a confident single 
  index with a sharp penalty drop for an abrupt break, but will either return
  nothing or smear many weak candidates across a gradual drift.
  - A windowed two-sample drift test (KS/PSI, reference vs recent) fires for
  both ‚ÄĒ but if you slide the reference window, an abrupt break flips the test
  statistic suddenly at one boundary, whereas drift makes it climb gradually.

  So you can diagnose by agreement: changepoint detector fires sharply +
  residual steps ‚Üí changepoint. Drift test climbs gradually + changepoint
  detector is unconfident + residual ramps ‚Üí drift.

  The honest caveat ‚ÄĒ it's a spectrum, not a binary. A "fast drift" over 5
  points and a "soft changepoint" smeared over 5 points are genuinely the same
  thing; the label depends on a window/timescale threshold you choose. This
  matters for your project in two ways:
  - Your generator should parameterize the transition width (őĒt). őĒt ‚Čą 1 ‚Üí
  changepoint; őĒt large ‚Üí drift; and you can deliberately generate the ambiguous
   middle to test whether the agent (and the tools) handle it gracefully.
  - The proposal already nods at this (¬ß2.5.3: "a single extreme point may
  justify outlier treatment; a persistent shift should be treated as drift or a
  changepoint"). The agent's interpretation of which one it is ‚ÄĒ and admitting
  uncertainty in the boundary cases ‚ÄĒ is part of what the evals grade.

  This is also a natural coordination point with the changepoint team: you two
  should agree on the transition-width convention so your synthetic cases don't
  overlap ambiguously, and so the agent has a consistent rule for labeling.
